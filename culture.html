Festival of the Month

Buddha Jayanti: Celebrating the Enlightened One

May marks one of Nepal's most sacred celebrations—Buddha Jayanti, commemorating the birth, enlightenment, and passing of Siddhartha Gautama, who was born in Lumbini, Nepal.

Thousands of pilgrims gather at Boudhanath and Swayambhunath stupas, lighting butter lamps and walking the sacred kora path. The air fills with incense, the sound of mantras, and a palpable sense of peace.

History & Heritage

Millennia of rich history shaped by diverse dynasties

~800 BCE - 300 CE

Kirat Dynasty

The earliest rulers of Nepal, known for establishing the foundation of Nepali society and culture. They worshipped nature and ancestors.

400 - 750 CE

Licchavi Period

A golden age of art and architecture. The famous Changu Narayan Temple dates from this era. Trade flourished with Tibet and India.

1200 - 1769 CE

Malla Dynasty

The era that gave us the stunning Durbar Squares of Kathmandu, Patan, and Bhaktapur. Arts, literature, and architecture flourished.

1769 - 2008 CE

Shah Dynasty

Prithvi Narayan Shah unified Nepal. The Shah kings ruled until 2008, seeing Nepal through tremendous political changes.

2008 - Present

Federal Democratic Republic

Nepal transitioned from a monarchy to a federal democratic republic, adopting a new constitution in 2015.

Architectural Marvels

Distinctive styles that define Nepal's cultural landscape

Pagoda Style

Originating in Nepal, this multi-tiered temple architecture later spread to China and Japan. The Nyatapola Temple in Bhaktapur is a stunning five-story example.

Shikhara Style

Influenced by Indian architecture, these conical spires grace temples like Krishna Mandir in Patan. The geometric precision is breathtaking.

Newari Woodcarving

The Newar artisans are masters of intricate woodwork—peacock windows, strut carvings, and toran gateways that tell stories of gods and demons.

Traditions & Attire

The vibrant clothing and customs that define Nepal's communities

Daura Suruwal

The national dress of Nepal, featuring a cross-front shirt and fitted trousers, often paired with a Dhaka topi.

Gunyo Cholo

The traditional women's attire—a sari-like garment paired with a blouse, adorned with stunning gold jewelry.

Sherpa Traditional Dress

Warm woolen chubas and colorful striped aprons designed for high-altitude living.

Tharu Heritage

The indigenous people of the Terai, known for their stick dances, distinctive tattoos, and longhouse living.

Festivals & Celebrations

"More festivals than days in a year"

Dashain

October (15 days)

Nepal's longest and most significant festival, celebrating the victory of good over evil. Families reunite, kites fill the sky, and elders give tika blessings.

Tihar

October/November (5 days)

The festival of lights honoring crows, dogs, cows, and siblings. Homes glow with oil lamps and colorful rangoli decorations.

Holi

March (2 days)

The exuberant festival of colors marking spring's arrival. Streets become canvases of joy as people splash each other with colored powder.

Chhath

October/November (4 days)

An ancient Vedic festival dedicated to the Sun God, observed primarily in the Terai. Devotees fast and stand in water at sunrise and sunset.

Religious Harmony

Where Hinduism and Buddhism embrace as one

Pashupatinath Temple

One of the holiest Hindu temples in the world, dedicated to Lord Shiva. The sacred Bagmati River flows alongside, where cremation ceremonies continue an ancient tradition.

Boudhanath Stupa

One of the largest spherical stupas in the world, the eyes of Buddha gaze in all four directions. Tibetan refugees have made this their spiritual home since 1959.

The Living Goddess: Kumari

Nepal is home to one of the world's most unique religious traditions—the worship of a living goddess. The Kumari is a prepubescent girl selected from the Newar Buddhist community who is believed to be the incarnation of the goddess Taleju.

Chosen through an ancient and rigorous selection process, she must possess 32 specific physical attributes and show no fear during tantric rituals. The Kumari lives in the ornate Kumari Ghar in Kathmandu's Durbar Square.

Her feet must never touch the ground outside her palace, and her public appearances are considered deeply auspicious. When she reaches puberty, she returns to normal life, and a new Kumari is selected.

The Bhojan (Cuisine)

Beyond the momo—discover Nepal's rich culinary heritage

Dal Bhat Power

The quintessential Nepali meal—lentil soup, rice, vegetables, pickles, and meat. "Dal Bhat power, 24 hour!"

Momo

Nepal's beloved dumplings, steamed or fried, filled with spiced meat or vegetables and served with fiery tomato achar.

Dhido

Traditional buckwheat or millet pudding, eaten with your hands alongside gundruk (fermented greens) and meat.

Yomari

A Newari delicacy—fish-shaped rice flour dumplings filled with sweet molasses and sesame, made during Yomari Punhi.

☕ Chiya Culture: More Than Just Tea

In Nepal, tea isn't just a beverage—it's a social institution. Whether it's a construction worker on a dusty road or a businessman in Kathmandu, everyone pauses for "chiya."

Ilam Tea: Grown in the misty hills of eastern Nepal, Ilam tea rivals Darjeeling in quality. The orthodox tea from this region is prized for its golden liquor and muscatel notes.

Masala Chiya: The everyday tea—black tea boiled with milk, sugar, and spices like cardamom, ginger, and cinnamon. It's served everywhere from mountain teahouses to city cafes.

The phrase "Chiya khanus" (please have tea) is the universal Nepali welcome.

Arts & Crafts

Centuries-old traditions kept alive by master artisans

Thangka Paintings

These intricate Buddhist scroll paintings depict deities, mandalas, and spiritual scenes. Artisans train for years to master the precise techniques passed down through generations.

Bhaktapur Pottery

Potter's Square in Bhaktapur has been producing traditional earthenware for centuries. Watch artisans shape clay on kick-wheels, just as their ancestors did.

The Khukuri

The iconic curved knife of the Gurkhas, a symbol of Nepali identity and bravery. Each blade is hand-forged and carries deep cultural significance.

The Sound of Nepal

Traditional instruments and melodies that echo through the valleys

Madal

The hand drum that forms the heartbeat of Nepali folk music. Its distinctive double-headed rhythm drives every celebration.

Sarangi

A bowed string instrument played by the Gandharba community, traditionally used by wandering minstrels to tell stories.

Bansuri

The bamboo flute, associated with Lord Krishna, creates the melodic soundscape of the Himalayan hills.
